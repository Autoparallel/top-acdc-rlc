\begin{abstract}
We develop a cohomological viewpoint on lumped electrical circuits, emphasizing how Kirchhoff-type laws and AC/DC behavior can be organized using (co)homology, exact sequences, and duality. We relate circuit topology to the existence of potentials and to the structure of solution spaces, and we outline how measurement can be interpreted via natural pairings between homology and cohomology.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Electrical circuits are often taught as an exercise in solving linear equations, but there is a persistent geometric theme: conservation laws look like exactness, potentials exist when cohomology vanishes, and interconnection is a gluing operation.
The goal of this note is to make that theme concrete for lumped RLC circuits and for idealized measurement devices.

We emphasize two complementary viewpoints:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a \emph{graph model} (a $1$-complex) capturing Kirchhoff constraints and terminal behavior, and
  \item a \emph{field-domain model} $U=S^3\setminus \mathrm{int}(M)$ capturing the topological degrees of freedom behind capacitance and inductance via Alexander duality and Poincar\'e--Lefschetz duality.
\end{itemize}
In both cases, numerical values (ohms, farads, henries) arise only after adding constitutive/geometric data, typically through a Hodge star and the associated energy/dissipation functionals \cite{jackson_em_1998,bott_tu_1982}.

\subsection{Background and prerequisites (light)}
\label{sec:background}
We assume basic familiarity with singular/cellular (co)homology, long exact sequences, and Poincar\'e duality; standard references include \cite{hatcher_at_2002,munkres_topology_2000}.
For the differential-form/Hodge-star viewpoint used to connect topology to energy pairings, see \cite{bott_tu_1982,morita_geometry_of_diff_forms_2001}.
For compositional/categorical language around passive networks, we draw inspiration from Baez--Fong and collaborators \cite{baez_fong_2015_compositional,baez_fong_pollard_2016}.

\section{Roadmap and scope}
This paper explains how circuit topology can be encoded by (co)homology and how standard circuit laws can be interpreted as exactness and duality statements.
The paper is organized in two layers: an introductory/expository layer that builds intuition and a more ``mathematical'' layer (definitions, precise statements, and worked topology examples).
Our main emphasis is:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a precise model of a lumped circuit as a (cell) complex, together with the relevant (co)chain complexes;
  \item Kirchhoff-type laws as boundary/coboundary constraints;
  \item AC forcing via periodic time ($S^1$) and the role of K\"unneth in organizing solution spaces;
  \item measurement as a pairing between homology and cohomology;
  \item higher-dimensional (co)homology in the field domain, explaining capacitance and inductance.
\end{itemize}

Readers primarily interested in the field-domain (Alexander/Poincar\'e--Lefschetz) perspective may skim the graph material and jump to the conductor/field-domain definitions, the DtN discussion, and the worked topology examples in \S\ref{sec:worked-topology-examples}.
Readers primarily interested in series/parallel laws should read the gluing section \S\ref{sec:gluing} alongside the DtN/Schur-complement discussion.

\subsection{Relation to Baez's work}
\label{sec:relation-baez}
This project is strongly influenced by John Baez's (and collaborators') viewpoint that circuit behavior is governed by topology together with linear algebraic structure (e.g.\ Dirichlet forms / energy functionals for resistive networks) and that network interconnection is best understood compositionally.
Baez's work is especially developed for \emph{resistive} circuits, where the key structure is a quadratic dissipation/energy functional whose minimization reproduces the usual laws for effective behavior at terminals.
See in particular \cite{baez_fong_2015_compositional,baez_fong_pollard_2016}.

Our emphasis differs in three main ways:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RLC (energy storage) rather than purely resistive behavior.}
  We extend the topological bookkeeping beyond purely dissipative Dirichlet-form structure to include \emph{inductive} and \emph{capacitive} degrees of freedom, which naturally live in higher-degree (co)homology of the field domain $U$ (and relative variants for terminals).
  \item \textbf{AC/DC signals via time topology.}
  We incorporate time dependence by products with $T=[0,\infty]$ (DC/transients) and $T=S^1$ (AC/steady periodic forcing), using K\"unneth-type decompositions to organize solution spaces and ``phasor'' degrees of freedom.
  A useful caution is that ordinary homology of $S^1$ only sees $H_0(S^1)\cong \mathbb{Z}$ (the constant component) and $H_1(S^1)\cong \mathbb{Z}$ (the winding class), so it does \emph{not} by itself encode the full harmonic spectrum of a periodic signal.
  The integer-indexed Fourier modes instead arise analytically from the characters of the circle group, i.e.\ the homomorphisms $S^1\to U(1)$ given by $e^{int}$ for $n\in\mathbb{Z}$ (Pontryagin duality), or equivalently as eigenfunctions of the Laplacian on $S^1$.
  \item \textbf{Measurement devices as constraints and pairings.}
  We model idealized voltmeters/ammeters/ohmmeters as imposing constraints on admissible configurations and extracting values via canonical pairings between homology and cohomology; this makes ``measurement is pairing'' part of the same exactness/duality framework as KCL/KVL.
\end{itemize}

We view these as complementary rather than competing perspectives: Baez's framework provides an elegant compositional and variational backbone for passive linear networks, while the present paper emphasizes the role of ambient $3$-dimensional topology (Alexander duality, linking, relative groups) and time topology in organizing the state space for RLC dynamics and measurement.

\section{The lumped graph model}
\label{sec:dictionary}
\subsection{Conventions and standing assumptions}
Unless stated otherwise, we work with real coefficients. We assume the graph $X$ is finite and oriented, and we use the cellular boundary map $\partial\colon C_1(X;\mathbb{R})\to C_0(X;\mathbb{R})$ and coboundary $d\colon C^0(X;\mathbb{R})\to C^1(X;\mathbb{R})$ with the usual sign conventions induced by the chosen orientations.
When discussing the field-domain model, we take $U$ to be an oriented compact $3$-manifold-with-boundary (typically a deformation retract of $S^3\setminus \mathrm{int}(M)$ after truncating ``at infinity'') and we single out a subset $\Gamma\subset \partial U$ representing terminals where boundary conditions and/or measurement probes are imposed.

\subsection{Basic objects: chains, cochains, and terminal data}
\paragraph{Graph model.}
A lumped circuit is modeled as a finite $1$-dimensional CW complex (equivalently a graph)
\[
X=(V,E),
\]
with vertices $V$ (junctions) and oriented edges $E$ (branches).
We write $C_k(X;\mathbb{R})$ for cellular chain groups and $\partial\colon C_1\to C_0$ for the boundary.
The dual cochains are $C^k(X;\mathbb{R})=\mathrm{Hom}(C_k(X;\mathbb{R}),\mathbb{R})$ with coboundary $d\colon C^0\to C^1$.

\paragraph{Terminal data (lumped).}
To talk about ``a device'' rather than ``a closed circuit'', it is useful to specify a set of terminals.
At the graph level we may either (i) fix a distinguished subset of vertices $T\subset V$ (terminal nodes), or (ii) work in a relative setting $(X,T)$ so that voltages/currents between terminals become relative (co)chains.

\subsection{Kirchhoff laws as exactness statements}
With the identifications above:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{KCL:} $\partial i=0$, i.e.\ branch currents form a $1$-cycle.
  \item \textbf{KVL:} $dv=0$, i.e.\ branch voltages form a $1$-cocycle.
\end{itemize}
Equivalently, $dv=0$ says that the net voltage around any $1$-cycle $\gamma\in Z_1(X;\mathbb{R})$ vanishes: $v(\gamma)=0$.
If $H^1(X;\mathbb{R})=0$ (for a connected graph, this is equivalent to $X$ being a tree), then every cocycle is exact, so $dv=0$ implies $v=d\phi$ for some node potential $\phi$.

\subsection{A first worked example: trees versus loops}
\label{sec:example-tree-loop}
Let $X$ be a connected graph.
\begin{itemize}
  \item If $X$ is a tree, then $H^1(X;\mathbb{R})=0$. Hence every voltage assignment satisfying KVL is of the form $v=d\phi$, i.e.\ there is a globally defined node potential.
  \item If $X$ has one independent loop (rank-$1$ cycle space), then $H^1(X;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}$. In this case, KVL implies $v$ is a cocycle, but there is in general a one-dimensional obstruction to writing $v=d\phi$; this obstruction is the cohomology class $[v]\in H^1(X;\mathbb{R})$ and can be detected by integrating $v$ around a fundamental cycle.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Components as constitutive relations}
Topology encodes constraints (cycles/cocycles), while elements encode relations between effort and flow variables. In lumped form, an element specifies a relation between branch voltage $v\in C^1(X;\mathbb{R})$ and branch current $i\in C_1(X;\mathbb{R})$, typically after choosing an inner product identifying $C_1\cong C^1$.

\subsection{Measurement as pairing}
Measured quantities are pairings between chains and cochains. For example, evaluating $v\in C^1(X;\mathbb{R})$ on a $1$-chain $\gamma\in C_1(X;\mathbb{R})$ produces the net voltage $v(\gamma)$ along a chosen path.

\paragraph{Idealized voltmeter/ammeter as constraint + pairing (graph model).}
At the lumped level, an ``ideal'' probe can be modeled by (i) adding an auxiliary edge representing the probe connection (or choosing a chain/path in the existing complex), (ii) imposing a constitutive constraint that captures ``do not disturb'', and (iii) evaluating the relevant chain--cochain pairing.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Voltmeter:} model as an added edge with effectively infinite resistance, so that the added branch current is constrained to be $0$ (open circuit). The readout is a voltage, i.e.\ evaluation of the circuit $1$-cochain $v$ on a chosen $1$-chain connecting the probe terminals.
  \item \emph{Ammeter:} model as an added edge with effectively zero voltage drop, i.e.\ constrain the corresponding branch voltage to vanish (short circuit). The readout is a current, i.e.\ evaluation of the circuit $1$-chain $i$ on that probe edge (or on a cut whose current is being measured).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Measurement in the field-domain model (Poincar\'e--Lefschetz + Hodge)}
\label{sec:measurement-hodge}
In idealized measurement, a device both (i) imposes a constraint on admissible fields/currents (``do not disturb the circuit''), and (ii) extracts a number as a canonical pairing.
Topology supplies the relevant (co)homology pairings (Poincar\'e--Lefschetz); geometry/constitutive data supplies a Hodge star which turns those pairings into inner products and hence into concrete readouts.

\paragraph{Poincar\'e--Lefschetz duality as the topological pairing behind ``readout''.}
For an oriented compact $3$-manifold-with-boundary $U$ and an appropriate choice of terminal boundary $\Gamma\subset \partial U$, Poincar\'e--Lefschetz duality provides perfect pairings of the schematic form
\[
H^k(U,\Gamma;\mathbb{R})\times H_{3-k}(U,\partial U\setminus \Gamma;\mathbb{R})\longrightarrow \mathbb{R},
\]
given by evaluation/integration.
In circuit language, this is the abstract statement that ``effort''-type quantities (cohomology classes of differential forms) are evaluated on ``flow''-type quantities (homology classes of cycles/chains), and measurement is an instance of this evaluation.

\paragraph{Hodge star as a constitutive identification (metric-dependent, but canonical once fixed).}
Once a metric and material data are chosen, the Hodge/constitutive operator $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ provides an isomorphism between $k$-forms and $(3-k)$-forms, and induces an inner product
\[
\langle \alpha,\beta\rangle_{\mathrm{mat}}:=\int_U \alpha\wedge (\star_{\mathrm{mat}}\beta).
\]
This is the analytic mechanism that turns ``pairing'' into ``dot product'', allowing one to speak of orthogonality, projection, and ``least disturbance'' principles for measurement probes (minimization of an energy or dissipation functional).

\paragraph{Voltmeter as a cohomology class and a pairing.}
An ideal voltmeter measures a potential difference between two terminals while drawing negligible current.
Topologically, a choice of measurement leads corresponds to a relative $1$-cycle (a path between terminals) $\gamma\in H_1(U,\partial U\setminus \Gamma)$, and a voltage field corresponds to a (relative) cohomology class $[E]\in H^1(U,\Gamma)$ (or to a cocycle $v\in C^1(X)$ in the lumped model).
The readout is the pairing
\[
\mathrm{Volt}([E],\gamma)=\int_\gamma E,
\]
which is well-defined on cohomology/homology classes precisely when the appropriate ``no magnetic induction in electrostatics'' hypothesis (closedness) holds.

\paragraph{Ammeter as a dual pairing (flux through a cut).}
An ideal ammeter measures current through a branch/cut while producing negligible voltage drop.
In the field picture, current is naturally represented by a $2$-form (current density) or its associated flux class; equivalently one measures the flux of $J$ across a spanning surface $S$ intersecting the conductor once:
\[
\mathrm{Amp}(J,S)=\int_S J.
\]
This is the same Poincar\'e--Lefschetz pairing but in complementary degree, and $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ provides the constitutive link between $E$-type and $J$-type quantities (e.g.\ Ohm's law $J=\sigma E$ in a conductor).

\paragraph{Ohmmeter as an induced bilinear form (Dirichlet-to-Neumann).}
An ideal ohmmeter applies a controlled boundary condition (voltage or current) on terminals and reads the response, thereby extracting an entry of the effective resistive operator.
Mathematically, this is the Dirichlet-to-Neumann map associated to the dissipation form $\int_U \sigma |E|^2\,dV$ (or its differential-form version), i.e.\ a map defined by minimizing energy subject to boundary constraints.
This recovers the usual ``effective resistance'' as a quadratic form on terminal data.

\paragraph{Remark: ``measurement is Hodge dual'' (what is meant).}
Informally, many measurements can be phrased as first applying a constitutive identification (a Hodge-type $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ turning fields into dual fluxes) and then evaluating on a topological test object (a cycle/cut).
The \emph{topological} part is Poincar\'e--Lefschetz pairing; the \emph{geometric/physical} part is the Hodge star.
Keeping these roles separate clarifies which features are invariant under topological deformations (existence of pairings, ranks of state spaces) and which depend on geometry/material (numerical readouts, calibration).

\subsection{A categorical viewpoint: measurements and effective elements as maps}
\label{sec:categorical-measurement}
Many ``interesting results'' in network theory become slick once one treats devices not as collections of equations but as \emph{maps between boundary data spaces}.
In our setting, those boundary data spaces are naturally finite-dimensional (co)homology groups associated to terminal surfaces, and the relevant maps are induced by:
\begin{itemize}
  \item restriction/pullback of (co)chains under inclusions of interfaces;
  \item Poincar\'e--Lefschetz evaluation pairings; and
  \item Hodge/constitutive operators turning evaluation pairings into quadratic forms.
\end{itemize}
Gluing devices corresponds to composing these maps, and Mayer--Vietoris/long exact sequences are the algebraic-topological mechanism ensuring the composition is well-defined.

\paragraph{Measurements as linear functionals.}
Idealized measurements are linear functionals on the appropriate boundary data space.
For example, a voltmeter is a functional on (classes of) electric 1-forms, while an ammeter is a functional on current flux classes.
At the topological level these functionals are induced by evaluation of cohomology classes on chosen test cycles/cuts; at the geometric level they can be realized as orthogonal projections with respect to the $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{\mathrm{mat}}$ inner product.

\paragraph{Effective elements as Dirichlet-to-Neumann type operators.}
A two-terminal ``black box'' defines a relation between terminal effort and terminal flow.
For linear passive devices this relation is encoded by a positive (semi)definite operator on a finite-dimensional terminal space, obtained by minimizing an energy/dissipation functional in the bulk subject to boundary constraints.
This is the manifold-level analogue of forming an effective resistance/capacitance/inductance by eliminating internal degrees of freedom (Schur complement).

\subsection{Higher-dimensional (co)homology for capacitance and inductance}
The graph $X$ is sufficient for KCL/KVL bookkeeping, but it cannot encode the topological degrees of freedom behind energy storage. Those live naturally in the topology of the field domain $U=S^3\setminus\mathrm{int}(M)$ and in relative groups involving $\partial U$.

\section{Conductors and constitutive data (rigorous setup)}
\label{sec:conductors-constitutive}
This section fixes the geometric objects (conductors, dielectric/magnetic regions) and the mathematical home for ``material parameters''.

\paragraph{Topology vs.\ numbers (executive summary).}
The topology of the conductor/field setup determines a finite-dimensional \emph{state space} of allowed charge/flux degrees of freedom (ranks of the relevant (co)homology groups, exact sequences, and duality pairings).
Choosing coefficients (typically $\mathbb{R}$, sometimes $\mathbb{C}$ for phasors) specifies the scalar ring/field in which these degrees of freedom and all resulting linear maps live.
Actual \emph{numerical} values of $R,L,C$ do not come from topology alone: they arise only after adding geometric/constitutive data (metric and material parameters) which induce bilinear forms/operators on that state space; $R,L,C$ are the matrices of those operators in physically meaningful bases.

\subsection{Conductor regions and the field domain}
\label{sec:conductor-region}
Let $S^3$ denote the one-point compactification of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

\begin{definition}[Conductor region]
A \emph{(perfect) conductor region} is a compact $3$-dimensional submanifold-with-boundary
\[
M\subset S^3
\]
whose connected components are the individual conducting bodies (wires, plates, coils, etc.).
We write
\[
\partial M=\bigsqcup_{\alpha=1}^m \Sigma_\alpha
\]
for its boundary components.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Field domain]
The \emph{field domain} (exterior region) is
\[
U:=S^3\setminus \mathrm{int}(M),
\]
which is a compact $3$-manifold-with-boundary with $\partial U=\partial M$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
This ``$S^3$-model'' is a clean way to avoid boundary conditions at spatial infinity; one can equally begin in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and impose decay conditions, but we will not need them for the topological statements below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Coefficients and what can (and cannot) live in cohomology}
\label{sec:coefficients}
The topology of $U$ produces free abelian groups $H^k(U;\mathbb{Z})$ and vector spaces $H^k(U;\mathbb{R})$; these describe \emph{which fluxes/charges/circulations are topologically possible}.
To obtain \emph{numerical} circuit parameters (ohms, farads, henries), one needs additional geometric/constitutive information (a ``metric'' and material tensors), which enters as operators on differential forms (a Hodge star / constitutive map), not as plain cohomology classes.

\paragraph{Constitutive maps (schematic).}
On the field domain $U$ we consider differential forms representing electromagnetic quantities, together with pointwise linear constitutive maps. In the simplest (linear, isotropic) case these are multiplication by scalar functions:
\[
\epsilon\colon U\to \mathbb{R}_{>0},\qquad \mu\colon U\to \mathbb{R}_{>0},\qquad \sigma\colon U\to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}.
\]
More generally one uses tensor fields, but we will not need that generality at the topological level.

\begin{remark}[What ``pops out'' where]
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \emph{choice of coefficient ring} (e.g.\ $\mathbb{Z}$ vs.\ $\mathbb{R}$) is a modeling choice for charge quantization vs.\ continuum approximations.
  \item Physical parameters like $\epsilon,\mu,\sigma$ are \emph{not} coefficients of singular cohomology; they are fields (functions/tensors) used to build bilinear forms on cohomology via integration.
  \item The actual lumped parameters ($C,L,R$) appear as entries of matrices representing these bilinear forms after choosing topological bases of the relevant (co)homology groups.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Relation to Baez's work (field-domain perspective)}
\label{sec:relation-baez-field}
The Dirichlet-to-Neumann and ``black-box'' emphasis in this section is closely aligned with the compositional/variational viewpoint developed by Baez--Fong and collaborators for passive linear networks, especially in the resistive case.
We refer the reader to \S\ref{sec:relation-baez} for a broader comparison; here we mainly use the field-domain perspective to highlight where ambient $3$-dimensional topology (Alexander duality, linking, relative groups) supplies additional structure in the RLC setting.

\subsection{Terminal components and relative (co)homology}
\label{sec:terminals}
To model a component (e.g.\ a capacitor) one must specify which conductor boundary components are treated as \emph{terminals}.
Accordingly, we allow a decomposition of the boundary
\[
\partial M = \Gamma \sqcup \Gamma_{\mathrm{rest}},
\]
where $\Gamma$ is the union of terminal surfaces (often a disjoint union of two components for a $2$-terminal element).
Relative groups such as $H^\bullet(U,\Gamma;\,R)$ and $H_\bullet(U,\Gamma;\,R)$ encode fields/fluxes subject to boundary conditions on terminals.

\subsection{From topology to lumped elements: forms, pairings, and matrices}
\label{sec:elements-as-pairings}
Fix a commutative ring/field $R$ (typically $\mathbb{Z}$ or $\mathbb{R}$).
Topological degrees of freedom appear as (co)homology classes; constitutive data turns them into numbers by integration pairings.
Concretely, one obtains symmetric bilinear forms (hence matrices after choosing bases) such as
\[
H^k(U;\mathbb{R})\times H^k(U;\mathbb{R})\to \mathbb{R},
\qquad
([\alpha],[\beta])\mapsto \int_U \alpha\wedge (\star_{\mathrm{mat}}\beta),
\]
where $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ is the Hodge/constitutive operator built from the Euclidean metric and $\epsilon$ or $\mu$ (depending on whether one is in the electrostatic or magnetostatic regime).
The capacitance and inductance \emph{matrices} are precisely the matrices of these forms in physically meaningful bases (charges/fluxes on terminal components and loops).

\subsection{Almost topological degrees of freedom (spectral and defect viewpoints)}
\label{sec:almost-topological}
The idealized homological/cohomological degrees of freedom discussed above are defined by \emph{exact} closedness and exactness conditions (e.g.\ $d\alpha=0$ and $\alpha\notin \mathrm{im}\,d$), and by perfect boundary conditions at terminals.
In physical circuits and laboratory geometries those ideals are never met exactly: conductors have finite conductivity, dielectrics leak, boundaries are imperfect, and coils are not literal topological links.
This section records several compatible notions of ``almost capacitive'' and ``almost inductive'' behavior that make the ideal topological picture quantitative.

\paragraph{Setup: a material inner product and a defect norm.}
Fix a constitutive Hodge operator $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ (built from the Euclidean metric and material parameters such as $\epsilon$ and $\mu$) and define the associated $L^2$-type inner product on forms by
\[
\langle \alpha,\beta\rangle_{\mathrm{mat}}:=\int_U \alpha\wedge(\star_{\mathrm{mat}}\beta).
\]
This makes spaces of $k$-forms into Hilbert spaces after completion, and provides a canonical notion of ``smallness'' for $d\alpha$ and for distance to subspaces such as $\mathrm{im}\,d$.

\paragraph{(I) Spectral viewpoint: ``almost'' = small eigenvalues of a (weighted) Hodge Laplacian.}
Let $\Delta_{\mathrm{mat}}$ denote the Hodge Laplacian on $k$-forms with the boundary conditions appropriate to the device under study (for example, relative boundary conditions on a terminal subset $\Gamma$).
In the ideal case, Hodge theory identifies the space of harmonic forms $\ker(\Delta_{\mathrm{mat}})$ with the corresponding (relative) cohomology group.
In practice, non-idealities perturb the kernel into a small but nonzero spectrum.
Thus one may define an ``$\varepsilon$-topological'' $k$-mode as a nonzero form $\alpha$ with small Rayleigh quotient
\[
\lambda(\alpha)\;:=\;\frac{\langle \Delta_{\mathrm{mat}}\alpha,\alpha\rangle_{\mathrm{mat}}}{\langle \alpha,\alpha\rangle_{\mathrm{mat}}}\;\le\;\varepsilon.
\]
An ``almost capacitive'' degree of freedom corresponds to an $\varepsilon$-topological mode in the degree governing electrostatics (typically $k=2$ in the field-domain bookkeeping), while an ``almost inductive'' degree of freedom corresponds to an $\varepsilon$-topological mode in the magnetostatic degree (typically $k=1$).
Physically, small eigenvalues correspond to slowly decaying modes (large time constants, high-$Q$ behavior, small leakage), which is exactly the engineering content of ``almost conserved'' stored charge/flux.

\paragraph{(II) Defect viewpoint: ``almost closed'' and ``almost exact''.}
Topology separates ``constraints'' (closedness) from ``potentials'' (exactness).
In a non-ideal device, one may quantify violations using the same inner product:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A form is \emph{$\varepsilon$-closed} if $\|d\alpha\|_{\mathrm{mat}}\le \varepsilon\|\alpha\|_{\mathrm{mat}}$.
  \item A form is \emph{$\varepsilon$-exact} if $\mathrm{dist}(\alpha,\mathrm{im}\,d)\le \varepsilon\|\alpha\|_{\mathrm{mat}}$.
\end{itemize}
This is a coordinate-free version of ``KVL/KCL hold up to small leakage/induction terms'' and makes precise what it means for a would-be cohomology class to be realized only approximately in a device.

\paragraph{(III) Mutual inductance without linking: topological skeleton vs.\ analytic coupling.}
The complement topology provides robust integer data (linking number) that can be viewed as an ``integral skeleton'' of inductive coupling.
However, two current loops with linking number $0$ can still have nonzero mutual inductance when placed nearby.
In the field-domain formulation this is natural: mutual inductance is a \emph{continuous} bilinear form obtained by composing a Green-operator/solution map (depending on $\mu$ and the geometry) with the relevant pairings.
Topology controls which couplings are protected under deformation without crossing, while geometry controls the magnitude and allows nonzero couplings even when the discrete invariant vanishes.

\paragraph{Remark: ``almost'' also addresses realizability/embedding constraints.}
The ideal capacitor example often uses a spherical cavity or other clean separating surfaces in $S^3$.
Real wiring and packaging force punctures, leads, and imperfect separation, so the relevant topological classes are rarely realized as exact invariants on the nose.
The spectral/defect viewpoints above provide a robust replacement: what matters is not literal existence of an exact class, but the presence of a low-dimensional subspace of modes that are nearly harmonic (hence nearly conserved) in the metric/material inner product relevant at the operating frequency and scale.

\subsection{From ideal topology to engineering models: losses, frequency response, and parasitics}
\label{sec:engineering-bridge}
The preceding sections emphasize that topology organizes \emph{which} degrees of freedom exist and how they glue.
To connect this to everyday circuit engineering one needs three additional refinements:
(i) nonzero loss moves exact conservation to slow decay,
(ii) AC analysis promotes state spaces to complex phasor spaces and inserts universal $i\omega$ factors, and
(iii) ``parasitics'' arise as small couplings/extension data when an ideal decomposition is only approximately valid.
This section records these refinements at a conceptual level, phrased in the same operator language (Hodge inner products and DtN maps) used elsewhere in the paper.

\paragraph{(A) Loss as a perturbation of self-adjoint structure (kernel $\to$ near-kernel).}
In ideal electrostatics/magnetostatics, the relevant operators are typically self-adjoint and positive (semi)definite with respect to $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_{\mathrm{mat}}$, and the corresponding cohomology groups appear as harmonic kernels.
In a real device, one has loss mechanisms (finite wire resistance, dielectric loss, magnetic loss), which can be modeled by adding dissipative terms to the variational functionals or, in the frequency domain, by allowing complex material parameters (e.g.\ complex permittivity).
The mathematical upshot is that ``topological'' kernels are replaced by slowly decaying modes or small singular values: the topological state space remains a useful \emph{organizing principle}, but its physical manifestation is controlled by a spectral gap and by quality factors.

\paragraph{(B) Cohomology-valued impedance: DtN maps in the frequency domain.}
In AC steady state, one tensors the relevant real state spaces with $\mathbb{C}$ and works mode-by-mode at angular frequency $\omega$.
Constitutive laws insert universal $i\omega$ factors (e.g.\ $i = i\omega q$ for capacitors and $v = i\omega \Phi$ for inductors), so that an effective device is described by a \emph{complex} operator relating terminal effort and flow.
Equivalently, the Dirichlet-to-Neumann construction becomes frequency-dependent:
\[
\mathrm{DtN}(\omega)\colon \text{(terminal potentials/fields)} \longrightarrow \text{(terminal currents/fluxes)}.
\]
In a finite-dimensional terminal basis this is the familiar impedance/admittance matrix $Z(\omega)$ or $Y(\omega)$.
This places transfer-function language and phasor circuit analysis into the same framework as the field-domain DtN operators: topology chooses the terminal/state spaces, while $(i\omega)$ and the constitutive operators determine the complex frequency response.

\paragraph{(C) Parasitics as small couplings / extension data.}
In ideal lumped models one assumes that elements are perfectly localized and interact only through specified terminals.
Real devices violate this assumption through stray capacitance, mutual inductance, leakage paths, and distributed effects.
In the present language, parasitics arise because the ``intended'' decomposition into subdomains (or into a graph plus isolated field-domain elements) is not exact: the overlap regions and ambient space carry additional weakly coupled degrees of freedom.
One can model this by enlarging the state space (adding extra modes) or by treating the ideal terminal operator as perturbed by small off-diagonal blocks.
Conceptually, this is again an operator perturbation problem: small parasitic couplings shift eigenvalues, destroy exact direct-sum decompositions, and turn exact MV splittings into approximate ones.

\subsection{Effective parameters as Dirichlet-to-Neumann maps (Schur complements)}
\label{sec:dtn}
The central analytic move turning topology into a lumped parameter is the passage from a bulk quadratic functional to an \emph{effective boundary operator}.
This is a Dirichlet-to-Neumann (DtN) type construction: one fixes boundary data on terminals, solves (or minimizes) in the interior, and reads off the induced conjugate boundary quantity \cite{jackson_em_1998,chua_desoer_kuh_1987}.

\paragraph{Resistive DtN (effective conductance/resistance).}
For a conductive region with dissipation functional $\mathcal{D}(E)=\int_U \sigma |E|^2\,dV$ (with $E$ constrained by appropriate boundary conditions), the effective terminal operator is obtained by minimizing $\mathcal{D}$ subject to prescribed terminal potentials.
The resulting map ``terminal voltage $\mapsto$ terminal current'' is the DtN map, and effective resistance is the inverse of this map on the terminal subspace.

\paragraph{Capacitive DtN (capacitance matrix).}
In electrostatics, one minimizes the electric energy $\mathcal{E}(E)=\tfrac12\int_U \epsilon |E|^2\,dV$ subject to boundary potentials on the conductor components \cite{jackson_em_1998}.
The induced boundary charges define a linear map from terminal potentials to terminal charges; in a basis of terminal components this is the capacitance matrix.

\paragraph{Inductive DtN (inductance matrix).}
In magnetostatics, one minimizes magnetic energy $\mathcal{E}(B)=\tfrac12\int_U \mu^{-1} |B|^2\,dV$ (or the corresponding vector-potential functional) subject to prescribed currents/flux constraints \cite{jackson_em_1998}.
The induced fluxes define a linear map between terminal currents (or loop currents) and flux linkages; in a basis this is the inductance matrix.

\paragraph{Gluing = composition (Schur complements commute with interconnection).}
When two devices are glued along an internal interface, eliminating the internal degrees of freedom produces a Schur complement.
This is the operator-theoretic shadow of Mayer--Vietoris: the exact sequence tells you how the state space splits/constraints under gluing, while the Schur complement tells you how the quadratic form descends to the terminal space.
This is the conceptual explanation for why effective $R,L,C$ parameters obey series/parallel reduction rules.

\paragraph{Series/parallel laws (deferred).}
The detailed series/parallel reductions are best stated after we have introduced Mayer--Vietoris and the relevant exact sequences; see \S\ref{sec:gluing} and \S\ref{sec:mv-series-parallel} for a topological formulation, and interpret the resulting numerical rules as Schur complements of the corresponding DtN operators.

\subsection{Where resistance fits}
\label{sec:resistance}
Resistance is intrinsically dissipative and is most naturally formulated as a positive-semidefinite bilinear form on currents (or electric fields), built from the conductivity $\sigma$:
\[
\mathcal{D}(E,E)=\int_U \sigma\, |E|^2\, dV,
\]
or equivalently (in differential-form language) $\int_U E\wedge (\star_\sigma E)$.
In the lumped graph model, this becomes a diagonal (or sparse) matrix on $1$-cochains/1-chains once a basis of branches is chosen.

\section{Alexander duality and the topology of conductors}
\label{sec:alexander-duality}
Alexander duality is the basic bridge between the topology of the conductor region $M\subset S^3$ and the topology of the field domain $U=S^3\setminus\mathrm{int}(M)$.
It is the mechanism by which ``capacitor-type'' disconnectedness of $M$ and ``inductor-type'' linking/loop phenomena in $U$ become the same statement in different degrees.

\subsection{Statement (informal form)}
Let $A\subset S^3$ be a compact, locally contractible subset (in particular, a compact submanifold-with-boundary as in our conductor model).
Then Alexander duality provides natural isomorphisms for reduced (co)homology:
\[
\widetilde H^k(S^3\setminus A;\,R)\;\cong\;\widetilde H_{2-k}(A;\,R),
\]
for all $k$, where $R$ is a coefficient ring/field.
In our setting, $A=M$ (or sometimes a deformation retract of $M$ capturing its topology).

\subsection{Two circuit-relevant consequences}
\paragraph{Capacitors (components of $M$).}
If $M$ has multiple connected components, then $\widetilde H_0(M;R)\neq 0$, hence by Alexander duality $\widetilde H^2(S^3\setminus M;R)\neq 0$.
This is the topological reason that separating $2$-classes in the field domain exist when there are multiple conductors: they support nontrivial electric-flux degrees of freedom (which become a capacitance matrix once $\epsilon$ is chosen).

\paragraph{Inductors (loops/linking in the complement).}
Nontrivial $\widetilde H_1(S^3\setminus M;R)$ (equivalently $H_1(U;R)$) corresponds dually to $\widetilde H^{1}(S^3\setminus M;R)\cong \widetilde H_{1}(M;R)$ in the simplest cases, and more generally is detected by the presence of nontrivial ``tunnels'' in the complement.
This is the topological home for magnetic flux/linking effects that, after choosing $\mu$, become the inductance matrix.

\subsection{Relative refinements and terminals}
For actual circuit elements we typically distinguish terminal surfaces $\Gamma\subset \partial M$ and impose boundary conditions there.
The correct statements then involve relative (co)homology and Poincar\'e--Lefschetz duality on $U$ (a manifold with boundary), together with excision to pass between $S^3\setminus M$ and $U$.
We will use these relative refinements when deriving capacitance/inductance matrices for multi-terminal devices.

\subsection{Alexander duality as a ``degree-of-freedom counter'' (and why it matches series/parallel)}
\label{sec:alexander-series-parallel}
Alexander duality is most useful here as a clean \emph{counting principle}: it predicts which finite-dimensional topological state spaces can exist before any constitutive geometry is chosen.
Combined with Mayer--Vietoris, it also explains why capacitor and inductor reductions naturally live in degrees $2$ and $1$, respectively, and why series/parallel can be expressed as direct sums versus constraints.

\paragraph{Capacitance degrees of freedom are dual to components of conductors.}
Write $M=\bigsqcup_{a=1}^m M_a$ for the conductor components.
Then $\widetilde H_0(M;R)\cong R^{m-1}$ measures the number of independent ways of assigning ``net charge differences'' between conductor components.
Alexander duality converts this into a statement about the complement:
\[
\widetilde H^2(U;R)\;\cong\;\widetilde H_0(M;R),
\]
so (up to the terminal/relative refinements used in actual devices) the electrostatic state space is a degree-$2$ cohomology group whose rank is controlled by how many conductor components there are.
This is the topological content of the slogan ``capacitors come from disconnected conductors.''

\paragraph{Inductance degrees of freedom are dual to loop topology of conductors (and/or tunnels in the complement).}
Similarly, $\widetilde H_1(M;R)$ detects independent loop/coil classes in the conducting region.
Alexander duality predicts corresponding degree-$1$ classes in the complement:
\[
\widetilde H^1(U;R)\;\cong\;\widetilde H_1(M;R).
\]
These are the cohomology classes that, when paired with $H_1(U;R)$ cycles, detect linking and thereby support magnetic flux/circulation degrees of freedom.
This is the topological content of ``inductors come from loops/linking.''

\paragraph{Why series/parallel gluing should look like direct sums vs.\ constraints.}
Series/parallel operations change the topology of $M$ (how conductor components are identified or connected), hence change $\widetilde H_0(M)$ and $\widetilde H_1(M)$ in controlled ways.
By Alexander duality, this forces corresponding changes in $\widetilde H^2(U)$ and $\widetilde H^1(U)$.
Mayer--Vietoris then describes those changes as either:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{direct sums} (when the overlap is homologically trivial in the relevant degree, giving isomorphisms like $H^k(U)\cong H^k(U_1)\oplus H^k(U_2)$), or
  \item \emph{kernels/quotients} (when gluing introduces an interface matching condition, so the global group is identified with a kernel of a restriction-difference map).
\end{itemize}
This is exactly the dichotomy that underlies the series/parallel laws once the energy forms are pushed through these splittings (additivity in the direct-sum case; minimization/Schur complement in the kernel/quotient case).

\paragraph{Capacitance.}
A capacitor requires multiple connected components of the conductor, i.e.\ nontrivial reduced $0$-homology $\widetilde H_0(M)$. Via Alexander duality this corresponds to $2$-dimensional topology in the complement: separating surfaces in $U$ support electric flux/charge degrees of freedom. With constitutive data (permittivity) this yields the capacitance matrix.

\paragraph{Inductance.}
Inductance is controlled by loop/linking topology, detected in $H_1(U)$ (and dually $H^2(U)$). Flux through spanning surfaces defines functionals on these classes, and mutual inductance is governed by the corresponding pairings (linking data in $U$). With constitutive data (permeability) this yields the inductance matrix.

\section{AC/DC signals via products with time and K\"unneth}
We model time dependence by taking a product with a $1$-manifold $T$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{DC} (transients/step response): $T=[0,\infty]$ (homeomorphic to an interval) with boundary $\partial T=\{0,\infty\}$.
  \item \emph{AC} (steady periodic forcing): $T=S^1$ with $\partial T=\emptyset$.
\end{itemize}
The relevant (co)homology of $T$ is elementary:
\[
H_0([0,\infty];\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R},\qquad H_1([0,\infty];\mathbb{R})=0,
\]
and
\[
H_0(S^1;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R},\qquad H_1(S^1;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}.
\]
For any space $Y$ used to model spatial/topological degrees of freedom (e.g.\ the circuit complex $X$ or a field domain $U$ with terminal data encoded by a pair), the product $Y\times T$ organizes time-dependent degrees of freedom. Over a field (here $\mathbb{R}$), the K\"unneth theorem gives a direct-sum decomposition
\[
H_k(Y\times T;\mathbb{R})\cong \bigoplus_{i+j=k} H_i(Y;\mathbb{R})\otimes H_j(T;\mathbb{R}),
\]
and similarly for cohomology. In particular, passing from DC ($T$ contractible) to AC ($T=S^1$) adds a new $H_1$-direction coming from the time circle, which is the homological shadow of ``phasor'' degrees of freedom.

\subsection{Topology-only constraints for DC vs.\ AC (what changes when time is $S^1$)}
\label{sec:dc-vs-ac-topology}
Even before choosing a Fourier basis, there is a purely topological distinction between DC (time contractible) and AC (time periodic) captured by K\"unneth.
If a spatial model $Y$ has nontrivial $H^{k}(Y;\mathbb{R})$, then passing to $Y\times S^1$ produces additional cohomology in degree $k+1$:
\[
H^{k+1}(Y\times S^1;\mathbb{R})\;\supset\; H^{k}(Y;\mathbb{R})\otimes H^{1}(S^1;\mathbb{R}).
\]
Thus any ``static'' cohomology class on $Y$ can be tensored with the time $1$-class to produce a new class on $Y\times S^1$.
This is a precise sense in which periodic forcing enlarges the natural cohomological state space of time-dependent configurations compared to the DC case.

\subsection{Leading/lagging and phase as ``$90^\circ$ rotation'' on the time circle}
\label{sec:leading-lagging}
The most visible dynamical signature of energy storage is phase shift: in sinusoidal steady state, ideal capacitors make current lead voltage, while ideal inductors make current lag voltage.
In this section we record the minimal algebra behind this phenomenon and point out how it fits naturally with the ``time as $S^1$'' picture.

\paragraph{Time derivatives and $S^1$-modes.}
Working on $T=S^1$ with coordinate $t$, the operator $\frac{d}{dt}$ acts on Fourier modes $e^{int}$ by multiplication by $in$.
Thus, in the frequency domain (or ``mode-by-mode''), differentiation corresponds to a $90^\circ$ phase shift.
This analytic fact is independent of circuit topology, but it is exactly what turns the topological state spaces (chains/cochains on $X$ or (co)homology classes on $U$) into \emph{phasor} state spaces when one tensors with $\mathbb{C}$.

\paragraph{Ideal capacitor.}
The constitutive law is
\[
i_C = C\,\frac{dv_C}{dt}.
\]
On a single $n$th Fourier mode (frequency $\omega=n$ in our normalization), this becomes
\[
i_C = (in)C\, v_C,
\]
so the impedance is $Z_C(n)=\frac{v_C}{i_C}=\frac{1}{inC}$ and the admittance is $Y_C(n)=inC$.
The factor $i$ produces a $+\frac{\pi}{2}$ phase shift: $i_C$ leads $v_C$ by $90^\circ$ (for $n>0$).

\paragraph{Ideal inductor.}
The constitutive law is
\[
v_L = L\,\frac{di_L}{dt}.
\]
Mode-by-mode this becomes
\[
v_L = (in)L\, i_L,
\]
so $Z_L(n)=inL$.
Again the factor $i$ produces a $+\frac{\pi}{2}$ phase shift in $v$ relative to $i$: equivalently $i_L$ lags $v_L$ by $90^\circ$ (for $n>0$).

\paragraph{How this interfaces with cohomological bookkeeping.}

The capacitor and inductor laws above can be read as \emph{operators in the $S^1$-direction} (multiplication by $in$ on the $n$th mode) composed with constitutive maps on the spatial/topological degrees of freedom.
Consequently:
\begin{itemize}
  \item the \emph{topology} (exactness constraints, ranks of state spaces, gluing laws) determines which modes are allowed and how they couple across the network, while
  \item the \emph{time-circle analysis} (the $in$ factor) is what produces the universal leading/lagging behavior characteristic of capacitors and inductors.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What is (and is not) ``topological'' about the $90^\circ$ shift.}
The existence of a capacitive or inductive \emph{state variable} is topological (it is detected by the appropriate (co)homology group being nontrivial, e.g.\ a rank-one piece of $H^2(U,\Gamma)$ for a two-terminal capacitor degree of freedom, or of $H^1(U)$ for an inductor degree of freedom).
However, the \emph{quadrature phase shift} itself does not come from (co)homology alone: it comes from the universal interconnection laws
\[
i=\frac{dq}{dt},\qquad v=\frac{d\Phi}{dt},
\]
which introduce a time derivative, hence an ``$i$'' in the frequency domain.
In this sense topology tells us \emph{that} there is a storage coordinate ($q$ or $\Phi$), while the time circle tells us \emph{how} it rotates effort/flow variables by $90^\circ$ on each mode.

\paragraph{Hodge star as a spatial duality (separate from the time rotation).}
The Hodge/constitutive operator $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ provides an additional duality on spatial forms: it identifies $k$-forms with $(3-k)$-forms and induces positive definite bilinear forms on cohomology by
\[
([\alpha],[\beta])\longmapsto \int_U \alpha\wedge (\star_{\mathrm{mat}}\beta).
\]
This is the mechanism that turns a purely topological degree of freedom into a \emph{numerical} capacitance or inductance (a stiffness/compliance in the quadratic energy).
But $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ is not responsible for the universal lead/lag sign: on each fixed Fourier mode, it acts as a real symmetric operator on the spatial state space, while the $90^\circ$ phase shift comes from the complex structure on $S^1$-modes (the $in$ factor).
Heuristically, one may think of ``two orthogonal rotations'' at play: a spatial duality provided by $\star_{\mathrm{mat}}$ and a time-circle rotation provided by $d/dt$ on $S^1$.

\subsection{Statements to be proved}
\label{sec:to-prove}
We will later justify the following statements in a precise setting.

\begin{theorem}[KCL and KVL as boundary/coboundary conditions]\label{thm:kcl-kvl}
For a finite $1$-complex $X$, KCL is the condition $\partial i=0$ on currents $i\in C_1(X;R)$ and KVL is the condition $dv=0$ on voltages $v\in C^1(X;R)$.
Moreover, $v$ is globally a gradient $v=d\phi$ if and only if its cohomology class vanishes in $H^1(X;R)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[AC adds time-circle degrees of freedom]\label{thm:ac-kunneth}
Let $Y$ be a space modeling spatial degrees of freedom (e.g.\ $X$ or a field domain pair $(U,\Gamma)$). Over a field, the (co)homology of $Y\times S^1$ decomposes as a direct sum of tensor products via K\"unneth, and the additional $H_1(S^1)$ factor accounts for steady periodic (phasor) degrees of freedom.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Capacitor/inductor degrees of freedom via Alexander duality]\label{thm:alexander-rlc}
For a conductor region $M\subset S^3$ with field domain $U=S^3\setminus \mathrm{int}(M)$, the groups controlling electrostatic (capacitance-type) and magnetostatic (inductance-type) degrees of freedom are naturally expressed in terms of reduced homology of $M$ and (co)homology of $U$, related by Alexander duality (and by relative Poincar\'e--Lefschetz duality when terminals are specified).
\end{theorem}


\section{Worked topology examples}
\label{sec:worked-topology-examples}
\subsection{Spherical cavity (absolute/relative homology)}
Let $M$ be the spherical shell obtained by removing the unit ball from the ball of radius $2$. Then $\partial M$ has two components, each a $2$-sphere, and the long exact sequence of the pair $(M,\partial M)$ computes the nontrivial groups:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $H_3(M,\partial M;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}$ generated by the fundamental class $[M]$.
  \item $H_2(\partial M;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}^2$ generated by the two boundary spheres; likewise $H_0(\partial M;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}^2$.
  \item $H_2(M;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}$ represented by a sphere separating the two boundary components.
  \item $H_1(M,\partial M;\mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}$ represented by a curve connecting the two boundary components.
\end{itemize}
This is the basic template behind ``capacitor'' behavior: disconnected boundary/terminal components and a separating $2$-class paired with a relative $1$-class.

\subsection{Linking, Gauss integrals, and mutual inductance (topological viewpoint)}
\label{sec:linking-mutual}
The simplest genuinely $3$-dimensional phenomenon behind inductors is \emph{linking}.
Topologically, linking is detected by homology/cohomology of the complement and by Alexander duality; analytically, it is computed by the Gauss linking integral.

\paragraph{Linking number.}
Let $\gamma_1,\gamma_2\subset S^3$ be two disjoint, oriented smooth embedded circles.
Their \emph{linking number} $\mathrm{Lk}(\gamma_1,\gamma_2)\in\mathbb{Z}$ can be defined topologically by choosing an oriented spanning surface $S$ with $\partial S=\gamma_1$ and setting
\[
\mathrm{Lk}(\gamma_1,\gamma_2):= \#(S\cap \gamma_2)
\]
as the signed intersection number (well-defined because $\gamma_2$ is disjoint from $\gamma_1$).

\paragraph{Gauss linking integral.}
If $\gamma_1,\gamma_2$ are parametrized in $\mathbb{R}^3$, the same integer is given by the Gauss integral
\[
\mathrm{Lk}(\gamma_1,\gamma_2)
=\frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{\gamma_1}\int_{\gamma_2}
\frac{(x-y)\cdot (dx\times dy)}{\|x-y\|^3}.
\]
This formula is a concrete instance of a cohomological pairing: it computes an intersection/linking pairing in the complement.

\paragraph{Mutual inductance as a geometrically weighted linking pairing.}
In magnetostatics, the flux through a loop induced by current in another loop is computed by a Biot--Savart-type integral.
When one passes to the cohomological formulation on the field domain $U$, mutual inductance becomes a symmetric bilinear form on the space of ``loop'' degrees of freedom (typically $H_1(U;\mathbb{R})$ or a relative variant), obtained by composing:
\begin{itemize}
  \item a topological pairing (linking / intersection, controlled by the complement topology), and
  \item a constitutive operator determined by $\mu$ (and the metric).
\end{itemize}
Thus, topology controls \emph{which} couplings can exist (and their integer skeleton via linking), while geometry/materials determine their numerical values.

\section{Gluing principles: excision and Mayer--Vietoris}
\label{sec:gluing}
Circuit building is a gluing operation: we form a larger device by joining subdevices along interfaces.
On the topological side, the corresponding bookkeeping tools are \emph{excision} and \emph{Mayer--Vietoris}.
This section records the basic statements we will use and explains their circuit interpretation at a high level.

\subsection{Excision (local modifications do not change relative groups)}
\label{sec:excision}
Excision says that relative (co)homology is insensitive to removing subsets away from the region where boundary conditions are imposed.
Informally, if $(X,A)$ is a pair and $B\subset A$ lies in the interior of $A$, then the inclusion
\[
(X\setminus B,\; A\setminus B)\hookrightarrow (X,A)
\]
induces isomorphisms on relative homology and cohomology.
In our setting, this justifies replacing complicated geometric conductor shapes by deformation retracts and simplifying the field domain $U$ away from terminals, as long as we keep fixed the terminal surfaces $\Gamma$ (or, more generally, the portion of $\partial U$ where conditions are imposed).

\subsection{Mayer--Vietoris (cut-and-paste exact sequences)}
\label{sec:mayervietoris}
Let $X=A\cup B$ be a union of subspaces with ``nice'' intersection $A\cap B$.
Then Mayer--Vietoris provides a long exact sequence relating $H_\bullet(X)$ to $H_\bullet(A)$, $H_\bullet(B)$, and $H_\bullet(A\cap B)$ (and similarly for cohomology).

\paragraph{Interpretation for circuits.}
If a device is decomposed into subdevices whose field domains overlap on an interface region, Mayer--Vietoris encodes the compatibility constraints and identifies how independent degrees of freedom in each piece combine (or are killed) in the union.
This is the correct algebraic-topological backbone for ``series/parallel''-type decompositions at the level of state spaces and exact sequences.

\subsection{Series/parallel as pushouts/pullbacks (optional viewpoint)}
At the lumped-graph level, series/parallel reductions are implemented by quotienting or identifying nodes/edges.
At the manifold/field level, the analogous operations are gluing along boundary components and tracking how the induced maps act on (co)homology; the exactness of Mayer--Vietoris is what guarantees consistent bookkeeping.
